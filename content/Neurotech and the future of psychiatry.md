When I was 16 years old, I made a threat on my life. It was much more of a cry for help than a legitimate attempt at death, but it got me admitted to an inpatient paediatric psychiatric facility. Upon admission I was asked many questions. Tell me about your childhood, your parents, your family, your friends. Tell me about school, and your teachers, and what you do when you are alone. These questions passed uneventfully. I had nothing of note to report. I was a normal teenage boy, who simply felt rot deep within his bones and wanted the pain to stop. The following morning I was promptly placed on the first line pharmacological treatment for depression in adolescents and begun daily therapy. It took me about 2 years, over 100 conversations with clinical psychologists, and over 10,000mg of fluoxetine to drag myself into the light once more. I can't help but feel that the future of this illness is going to look very different.

What are the disorders of the mind? Modern psychiatry is still very much in the throws of this question. If one squints, the answer is somewhat obvious: maladaptive neural circuits. Though the specifics differ between disorders and are likely multivariate. Like all features of biology, some weighted combination of genetic and environmental factors is responsible. What results is the perturbation of critical neural structures through cell death or pathogenic modulations in synaptic or receptor densities. These shifts in the connectome are subtle enough that they are not directly fatal, but rather they augment the phenomenology of the afflicted in unfortunate ways. Interestingly, they seem to cluster together, giving rise to the finite list of conditions found in the DSM.

For each label, there is a drug - or class of drugs. Depression was once thought to be the consequence of failures of the serotonergic system in the brain. Consequently, drugs for treating depression focused on reducing the extent to which the brain can re-absorb serotonin and similar neurotransmitters from their active location in extra-cellular space. As it turns out, the truth is slightly more complicated than that. We are now finding evidence for the the indirect effects of elevated extracellular serotonin on [suppressing subcortical circuits related to fear responses](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27874847/), and even [increasing the levels of proteins that modulate the growth of neurons and synapses](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25553404/). In general, this pattern follows for other disorders: identify  on a circuit involved in a specific cluster of symptoms, identify a receptor that is disproportionately expressed in this circuit, administer a drug that modulates activity for that receptor up or down as needed. ADHD medications potentiate frontal dopamine and norepinephrine, medications for psychosis suppress dopaminergic activity in the striatum, anxiety medications increase the effect of GABA globally. 

Pharmacology is a blunt instrument. If an ingested chemical is able to cross the blood-brain-barrier, it is generally made available to the entire brain.
